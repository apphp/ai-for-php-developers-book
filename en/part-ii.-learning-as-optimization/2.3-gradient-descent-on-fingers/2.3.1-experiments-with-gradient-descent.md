# 2.3.1 Experiments with gradient descent

**Why Look Inside the Training Process**

Up to this point, we've treated models as finished objects. There is data, there is a formula, and there is a result. Linear regression, for example, appears almost static once it's complete: we plug in the features, obtain the coefficients, and make predictions.

In reality, however, a model doesn't appear out of nowhere. Before its coefficients become "correct," they go through a long journey. That journey is what we call training.

Gradient descent is not just a formula or an abstract algorithm—it's a process. It consists of a sequence of small steps, each one adjusting the model's parameters slightly in the direction that reduces the error. To truly understand it, seeing only the final coefficients isn't enough. You need to watch how they move.

**From Models to Parameter Motion**

That's why, in this part of the book, we'll temporarily set aside business problems and real-world applications. Instead, we'll focus on the training process itself: how a parameter changes from one step to the next, how the error evolves, what happens when the learning rate is chosen well—or poorly—and why a model can sometimes appear to get "stuck."

These experiments are intentionally simple. They don't involve large datasets, sophisticated features, or advanced models. Instead, they make visible what is usually hidden inside the `train()` method: the direction of movement, the role of the derivative, and the effect of every single step.

The goal of this chapter is to build intuition. Once you've worked through these experiments, gradient descent will stop feeling like a mysterious algorithm and start looking like an ordinary, almost mechanical process. And once you understand the process, you also understand its limitations.

In the following sections, we'll walk through several classic experiments.

Each experiment is implemented in pure PHP and thoroughly commented so you can literally see and feel how gradient descent works from the inside.
